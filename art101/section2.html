Q1. Data is just simple numbers and values without much meaning on their own. When data is put together into something meaningful, then it becomes information. Data is essentially the building blocks of information. It has to be selected, linked, and conclusions must be drawn from sets of data for it to become information.

Q2. Mapping is a broader term of representing something by taking one value translated to another corresponding one of a different medium. Visualization is a subset of mapping that specifically deals with mapping data onto a visible representation.

Q3. The sublime that anti-sublime art opposes is referring to is representational art where you are showing a representative interpretation of something that already has a tangible representation. The anti-sublime art is showing representations for the things that can't be directly represented. I don't really agree based on this understanding. I don't think "sublime" is the right word to use. I think data representation through visualization can still be sublime or even certain data just in thinking about it can still be sublime. The transforming of data points into a meaningful representation can absolutely produce a sublime reaction. It doesn't even have to be big or bright. A simple graph showing the temperature spike of climate change or a visualization of the earth's size compared to the sun can be sublime for instance.

Q4. His work is about working together and visualization of individual behaviors put together into a single hub that people can view it from. In some of his work, the participants are aware of what they're contributing to, while in others they aren't. The most prevalent theme is individual contributions working together to create something greater. Some works for example are Johnny Cash, Sheep Market which put individuals' drawings made under certain guidelines together. He also has worked with Google on Chrome Experiments, which is a platform for Java, HTML5, WebGL, etc. experiments. It has free, open source tools and is a platform for these experiments that don't fit elsewhere. Within the context of the individual contributing to a larger group to make a work, he is stepping out of the box to create things that happen because of web for web. His music videos and world which are generated right in the browser are good examples of this.

Q5. Aaron Koblin's work deals with getting a bunch of unique, individual inputs created under certain guidelines contributing to a bigger piece.

Crowdsourcing, maybe software as platform.
